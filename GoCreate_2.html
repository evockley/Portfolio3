What is This?

For years, Men’s fashion, tailoring, and custom clothing have been on a growth trajectory. This App redesign is a solo passion project that updates an existing order system for salespeople.

Using the existing interface, custom clothing orders can take as long as 90 minutes to submit. I knew this problem intimately because I led the department that used the system. The order system was a negative touchpoint for users which ultimately led to a negative customer experience, too.

What I learned

This project challenged me to pivot from designing for a desktop to an iPad: I learned that even my “failed” iterations can be resurrected in unforseen circumstances.

Brief

The Problem

Custom clothing salespeople have an antiquated order system that is cumbersome, confusing, and difficult to use. Contextual inquiry has shown that it can take as long as 90 minues to submit a single order.

The frustrating experience effetively disincentivizes users from making sales in the department, thereby preventing the company to capitalize on its investment as anticipated.

Existing Fit Tools Interface

Redesigned Interface that caters to user needs

The Challenge

I had to retain the many details associated with custom clothing while allowing users to complete their tasks quickly, easily, and accurately.

The Solution

A highly organized, graphic apprach to order input, designed with speed, ease, and mobile technology in mind.

Discovery

Contextual Inquiry

I knew how these users behaved based on a year of contextual inquiry. Working with them as team lead gave me good insight to their problems and the effects on the business. But I still didn’t know their inner monologue.

User Interviews

I conducted interviews with three users to uncover their thoughts, feelings, motivations, and pain points.

Insights

Interviews taught me that users felt demotivated and stressed by the application. The excessive time it took to submit orders and the unintuitive interface felt like a punishment for making sales.

Imagine how that affected sales.

User Story

Quentin, a veteran custom clothing salesperson, met a prospective client, Jonathan, at a wine tasting event. Jonathan came in a few weeks later and spent a couple of hours shopping around and getting fitted for a new jacket. He mentioned that he needed a wardrobe overhaul as he had just lost a lot of weight.

Quentin wanted to get this order right because he takes pride in his work, but also because there were multiple sales on the line.

Problem Statement

Custom clothing salespeople who are busy and distracted need a way to communicate complex client orders quickly, accurately, and easily.

Hypothesis

I believed that decluttering the workspace for custom clothing salespeople would allow them to take control of their process, reduce order entry time, and reduce the likelihood of errors.

Ideation

The User Flow was iterated based on feedback from users. My initial attempt at a user flow included fewer screens as that was one of the main pain points from users. I created

I learned that the number of screens wasn’t really the issue. Users were happy to interact with more screens if there was less to do on each and fewer opportunities for error.

Early Sketches

Interviews revealed that users need more confidence in the system before they’re willing to trust it. It became imperative to design with ease, feeback, and error prevention in mind.

I hypothesized that I could eliminate much of the confusion of the existing interface by removing the screen clutter and making the experience more visual.

A search bar style interaction would allow a user to see only the specific alterations they need for each order

A visual interaction would be very cool, but too difficult to build and also introduce opportunities for error and confusion.

Yes, another search bar, but in imagining what the results might look like, I prepared myself to solve an unforseen problem. Read on!

I tested this quick wireframe early and learned that I needed to change my approach.

Wireframe

Between the time I began the project and this wireframe, the company purchased iPads for each employee. Now, rather than going to a back office and entering orders, users were expected to enter orders on their mobile devices.

This presented a new set of problems. In addition to the challenging interface, users needed to interact with it on a device with a smaller screen (no, it wasn’t responsive design), and with added disctractions to their workflow.

I needed to come up with a new solution.

Pivot

Luckily, my sketches included something that sort of looked like tappable cards, so I adapted that approach for an iPad. Now users were able to tap plus and minus icons to increase or decrease a measurement.

The most used alterations are on the first page, and users needing more obscure alteration can find them tucked away in tabs using standard industy language: Posture, Circumference, and Length.

A tappable interface with visual representations of the work to be done had more favorable test results.

Next Steps

I’ll implement what I learned from usability tests to clear up a few issues with this version. After that, I’ll keep working on the project to introduce some features that will address anticipated needs: what happens if a customer orders two jackets in the same purchase? What if there are 5 shirts that are all styled different using the same fit? What happens if a fabric is sold out?

These are all questions to be adressed in subsequent iterations so these users can have a great experience with their workflow.
