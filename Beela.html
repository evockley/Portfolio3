Brief

Problem

At the height of the Great Resignation, Beela, a Swedish nonprofit helping women and non-binary immigrants get into tech, asked Tech Fleet to help improve the way they served their members.

Challenge

Millions around the world recalibrated their careers, but Beela wasn’t seeing the growth or engagement they wanted. The provided several touch points through the career changing journey—a podcast, a mentorship program, a newsletter.

Our task was to examine career-changers’ experience with Beela and ideate concepts to improve the nonprofit’s services and convert more users to their mentorship program.

Solution

We created a dedicated Slack community with several channels, a way for new career changers to start comparing career paths, and a three-pronged feature on the homepage giving users what they need at the beginning, middle, and end of their career transition journey.

Logistics

  • I joined the project for Phase 2, where we
    • synthesized Phase 1 research
    • defined the opportunity space
    • ideated and tested solutions
  • I was a UX Researcher. However, Tech Fleet has a flexible structure, so I did some Design and Strategy work as well.
  • We ran six 1-week sprints, each with a different goal.

Constraints

  • Beela’s ultra-niche target audience made access to ideal interview subjects difficult.
  • Tech Fleet Apprenticeships are a 10 hour per week commitment. We frequently worked asynchronously throughout the day, but synchronous work time was hard to come by.
  • Beela's team is small and so is its development bandwidth. Our solutions had to be feasible for a 2 person volunteer team.

Background

In Phase 1, Tech Fleet set out to learn who makes up Beela’s community, what their journey into tech looks like, and why they join Beela. Here are some high-level findings from a survey distributed by Phase 1:

Who are they?

The Typical Beela user is a female immigrant to Sweden. She’s making the transition to tech, but despite being highly educated, she’s is not employed.

What does their immigration journey look like?

She’s moved to Sweden with a partner, and does not have children. Her Swedish is OK, but not great. This has resulted in career challenges, cultural barriers, and lack of a support system.

What does their journey into tech look like?

She’s interested in tech for personal fulfillment and career benefits, but fewer language challenges and gender equity are equally important. The hurdles she faces in her career change include knowing where to start, lacking industry knowledge, limited resources, self-doubt, and lack of belonging.

Why do they join Beela?

Beela shows up on her social media and seems interesting because of its mission and as a way to learn and make social connections. While she knows about some of the resources, she does not frequently use them.

Align

with client goals

In order to do the most effective and useful work possible, we began Phase 2 with an outcomes workshop with our client.

Why we did this: Focusing our efforts on one business goal allowed us to target our user-centered research and design efforts on activities that would support the most important business problem and focus on outcomes (increased revenue) rather than outputs (a new homepage).

What I did: Nonprofit revenue presentation

We met the Beela Stakeholders in Miro and asked them to individually create stickies with:

  1. Their priorities for Beela
  2. Their most important business outcomes

Next, we asked them to connect their priorities to the business outcome those priorities would drive.

Then, we did a quick grouping activities, labeled the groups, and asked them to prioritize the labeled groups in order of importance

The stakeholders prioritized REVENUE.

Revenue Models: As a result of this workshop, I researched and presented a handful of possible nonprofit revenue models for Beela to consider. Different models relied on support from individuals, companies, governments, and former benefactors to varying degrees. The unifying trait of the models? Results.

What We Learned: The workshop resulted in a clear understanding that we needed to help Beela generate revenue. This was a challenge because of Beela’s commitment to provide free services to users.

Beela would need to base their fundraising on Key Performance Indicators that spoke to their success, such as placement rates, conversion rates, number of members, duration of membership, etc.

Synthesize

Phase 1 Research

In addition to a survey, Phase 1 conducted several interviews. We affinity mapped the findings, identified pain points, and turned them into business opportunities. Once the data was organized, we asked stakeholders to prioritize the opportunities we presented.

Why we did this: To keep users front and center in our process, to have a structured framework to refer back to in later sprints, to communicate our work in a way that stakeholders would understand.

What I did: Individual Affinity Map, Collaborated on Team Affinity Map

Affinity Map: Each team member asynchronously affinity mapped the sticky notes from Phase 1. This allowed us to identify trends on our own before coming together to discuss. We all saw hot spots around finding community, getting support, and questions about how or where to start.

What We Learned: Users have various goals and pain points around starting a new career, but those goals overlapped at a high level.

Identfy

Opportunities

While the Affinity Mapping work helped us identify trends in research findings, it didn’t put those trends within a business context. The Opportunity Solution Tree, described here by Teresa Torres, was our chance to do just that: filter business opportunities through user needs and goals, then prioritize those user-centric opportunities based on what would help the business. win-win!

Opportunity Solution Tree (OST): This framework helped combine user and business goals. The root of the tree is the main need Beela addresses: career transitioners want to find a job in tech. The subsequent levels of branches are distinct opportunity areas that help users meet that goal, each with its own set of supporting opportunities.

Each team member created their own OST asynchronously:

Ultimately, the team collaborated to consolidate our OSTs into a single document we would use for the duration of the project:

I need help

  • with the job application process
  • understanding and navigating the tech transition process
  • choosing a career path within tech

I want

  • someone supporting me through this transition emotionally
  • to be/feel qualified to get a job in tech

Opportunity Prioritization: We asked stakeholders to help us prioritize the opportunities we had identified. We asked them to think about which branches they currently address and which would have the greatest impact if they were addressed right now.

Map

The Journey

We followed Google Design Sprint methodology to develop a high level service blueprint. To do that, we mapped, brainstormed, sketched, and voted on ideas.

Why we did this: Originally, our goal for the week was to create a testable prototype for the first prioritized branch of the Opportunity Solution Tree: I need help understanding and navigating the tech transition process. However, as we sketched solutions, we found that a service blueprint was a better deliverable than a prototype at this stage.

What I did: "How Might We..." questions, sketching, voting on sketches, journey mapping

Brainstorm: First, we created a bunch of “How Might We…” questions and grouped similar stickies. 12 distinct categories emerged. We voted first on what we thought was the most important theme to focus on, and then on the most important question within that theme to arrive at our design challenge:

How might we provide differentiated resources to users depending on their needs or stage of career transition?

Map: With a design challenge identified, we created a (really) rough journey map of a simple use case of a first time user trying to find resources about the transition process.

We highlighted what we thought was the most important step in the process for us to work on (find ways to engage or absorb information).

Sketch: We tackled this design challenge asynchronously in two meetups based on team availability. To structure our time, we used the 6-1-1 model.

My sketches focused on ways to get users from point A to point B. I used strategies like a progress bar, a checklist, and navigation tabs to allow users to see the various pieces of information related to each step.

As we talked through our sketches, we took notes on the UI components we described. It was more important for us to think about what the UI in these sketches was doing than how it looked.

We realized that despite very different layouts, the team had several different versions of visualized steps of the transition journey, breaking up the scary career transition journey into manageable steps.

Customer Journey Map: Having alignment on visualized steps to a career in tech allowed us to zoom out and think about a Customer Journey Map. We believed this would give us the clarity we needed about what resources a user could need at each step of their journey.
